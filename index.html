FINANCIAL VIABILITY ANALYZER
// objective financial analysis

Does the math
back your gut?

Enter the raw numbers from Yahoo Finance. The tool computes 18 financial ratios, scores each against professional benchmarks, and tells you whether the financials support an investment thesis.

NOT financial advice. Benchmarks are generalised across all industries — always compare to direct sector peers. This tool scores point-in-time financials only. It does not capture trend direction, competitive dynamics, or macro conditions.
1
Income Statement
Revenue · Margins · Earnings
›
iYahoo Finance → search the ticker → click Financials → select Income Statement → switch to Annual. Enter all figures in the same unit (all in $M or all in $B — be consistent throughout).
Revenue — Year 1 (most recent)
Total income from selling products or services before any costs. The topmost line of the income statement. Used to compute all margin ratios and growth rates.
Income Statement → Total Revenue → most recent year
Use consistent units throughout — $M or $B
Revenue — Year 2 (prior year)
The year before Year 1. Used to compute year-over-year revenue growth rate — the most watched growth metric.
Income Statement → Total Revenue → prior year column
Revenue — Year 3 (two years prior)
Two years before Year 1. Together with Year 1, used to compute the 2-year compound annual growth rate (CAGR) — a smoother measure less affected by one anomalous year.
Income Statement → Total Revenue → two years prior
Gross Profit
Revenue minus the direct cost of producing goods or services (cost of goods sold). Gross Margin = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue. This is the single most important margin — it reveals pricing power and the fundamental economics of the product. High gross margin = room to invest in growth, absorb costs, and generate profit.
Income Statement → Gross Profit
Operating Income (EBIT)
Profit from core business operations — after deducting cost of goods sold AND operating expenses (R&D, sales, admin) but before interest and taxes. Operating Margin = EBIT ÷ Revenue. This shows whether the actual business model is profitable, independent of how the company is financed.
Income Statement → Operating Income or Total Operating Income
Net Income
The final bottom-line profit after all expenses, interest, and taxes. Net Margin = Net Income ÷ Revenue. This is what actually accrues to shareholders. Consistently compare it to Free Cash Flow — if net income is high but FCF is low, earnings quality is suspect.
Income Statement → Net Income (at the bottom)
EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation and Amortisation. A widely used proxy for cash operating profit, essential for the Net Debt / EBITDA leverage ratio. Listed directly in Yahoo's income statement, or found by adding back D&A to Operating Income.
Income Statement → EBITDA (scroll down)
Interest Expense
The annual cost of servicing debt — interest payments to bondholders and lenders. Used to compute Interest Coverage Ratio = EBIT ÷ Interest Expense. A ratio below 2x means most operating profit is consumed by interest payments, leaving almost no cushion if earnings dip. Enter as a positive number even though it appears negative in the statement.
Income Statement → Interest Expense (enter as positive)
2
Balance Sheet
Cash · Debt · Equity · Liquidity
›
iYahoo Finance → Financials → Balance Sheet → Annual. Use the most recent year. Numbers should match the same unit as your income statement entries above.
Cash & Short-term Investments
All cash the company holds plus short-term investments that can be quickly liquidated. Subtracted from Total Debt to get Net Debt. A large cash balance relative to debt is a fortress signal — it means the company can survive a downturn and self-fund growth without needing external capital.
Balance Sheet → Cash And Cash Equivalents (or Cash And Short Term Investments)
Total Debt
All interest-bearing borrowings — both short-term debt (due within 12 months) and long-term debt. Net Debt = Total Debt − Cash. Net Debt / EBITDA above 3.5x is elevated; above 5x is dangerous. Yahoo lists "Total Debt" directly, or you can sum "Long Term Debt" and "Current Portion of Long Term Debt".
Balance Sheet → Total Debt (or Long Term Debt + Current Debt)
Total Assets
Everything the company owns: cash, accounts receivable, inventory, property, equipment, intangibles, and goodwill. Used to compute Return on Assets (ROA = Net Income ÷ Total Assets), which measures how efficiently the company generates profit from its asset base.
Balance Sheet → Total Assets
Total Shareholders' Equity
What shareholders own after subtracting all liabilities from all assets. Used to compute Return on Equity (ROE = Net Income ÷ Equity) and Debt-to-Equity ratio. Listed as "Total Stockholders' Equity" or "Total Equity". Note: companies that buy back a lot of stock or have significant losses can show negative equity — enter with a negative sign if so.
Balance Sheet → Total Stockholders' Equity (bottom of section)
Total Current Assets
Assets expected to be converted to cash within the next 12 months: cash, accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses. The numerator of the Current Ratio, which measures whether the company can cover near-term obligations without raising new capital.
Balance Sheet → Total Current Assets
Total Current Liabilities
Obligations due within 12 months: accounts payable, short-term debt, deferred revenue, accrued expenses. Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities. Below 1.0 means the company cannot cover near-term obligations from existing current assets alone — a liquidity warning.
Balance Sheet → Total Current Liabilities
3
Cash Flow Statement
Operating CF · CapEx · Free Cash Flow
›
iYahoo Finance → Financials → Cash Flow → Annual. This is the most reliable financial statement — cash is significantly harder to manipulate than earnings through accounting choices.
Operating Cash Flow
Cash actually generated from running the business — the purest measure of operational health. Consistently lower than Net Income is a major red flag (earnings may be inflated through accounting). Consistently higher is a green flag (earnings quality is high). Listed as "Operating Cash Flow" or "Cash From Operations" at the top of the cash flow statement.
Cash Flow → Operating Cash Flow (top section)
Capital Expenditure (CapEx)
Cash spent to maintain or expand physical assets — factories, equipment, servers, real estate. Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow − CapEx. CapEx appears as a negative number on Yahoo; enter it here as a positive number. High CapEx relative to OCF means the business needs heavy reinvestment to sustain itself, leaving less free cash.
Cash Flow → Capital Expenditure (enter as positive)
iThe tool automatically computes Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow − CapEx, and FCF Margin = FCF ÷ Revenue. Yahoo sometimes displays Free Cash Flow directly at the bottom of the Cash Flow section — you can use it to verify the computation.
4
Valuation Multiples
P/E · EV/EBITDA · P/S · P/B
›
iYahoo Finance → search ticker → click Statistics tab → look under Valuation Measures. All multiples are pre-computed. These are inherently relative — always compare to direct industry peers, not just the benchmarks below.
Trailing P/E Ratio
Share price divided by the last 12 months of actual earnings per share. Shows how many dollars investors are paying for each dollar of real earnings. A high P/E = high growth expectations baked in — any miss triggers sharp selloffs. N/A if the company is loss-making.
Statistics → Valuation Measures → Trailing P/E
Forward P/E Ratio
Share price divided by next 12 months' estimated earnings. If forward P/E is meaningfully lower than trailing, analysts expect earnings growth. If forward P/E is higher than trailing, earnings are expected to shrink. A large spread between the two warrants investigation into what is driving the change.
Statistics → Valuation Measures → Forward P/E
EV / EBITDA
Enterprise Value (market cap + net debt) divided by EBITDA. Widely used to compare companies with different capital structures. More complete than P/E because it accounts for debt. Below 10x is generally considered inexpensive; above 20x requires strong growth to justify. Listed directly on Yahoo's Statistics page.
Statistics → Valuation Measures → Enterprise Value/EBITDA
Price / Sales (P/S)
Market cap divided by annual revenue. Useful for pre-profit companies where P/E is meaningless. Software companies often trade at high P/S (10–30x) due to high margins and scalability. A manufacturing company at 5x P/S would be very expensive. Context is everything here.
Statistics → Valuation Measures → Price/Sales (ttm)
Price / Book (P/B)
Share price divided by book value per share (equity per share). Below 1.0 means the market values the company below its net assets — either a bargain or a sign of structural deterioration. Above 5x is expensive unless ROE is very high. Banks and financial companies are traditionally evaluated heavily on P/B.
Statistics → Valuation Measures → Price/Book (mrq)
5
Returns & Efficiency
ROE · ROA
›
iYahoo Finance → Statistics → Management Effectiveness section. These are pre-computed. If you prefer, leave them blank and the tool will compute them from the income statement and balance sheet figures you entered above.
Return on Equity (ROE)
Net Income ÷ Shareholders' Equity, expressed as a percentage. Measures how efficiently management uses equity capital to generate profit. Above 15% is solid; above 20% is excellent. Beware: very high ROE driven by heavy debt (low equity) is misleading — always check the leverage situation alongside ROE.
Statistics → Management Effectiveness → Return on Equity (ttm)
Enter as % (e.g. 147.25 for 147.25%). Leave blank to auto-compute from your inputs above.
Return on Assets (ROA)
Net Income ÷ Total Assets, expressed as a percentage. Measures how efficiently the company turns its entire asset base into profit. Above 5% is decent; above 10% is strong. Asset-heavy industries (manufacturing, utilities, airlines) naturally score lower than asset-light businesses (software, consulting).
Statistics → Management Effectiveness → Return on Assets (ttm)
Enter as % (e.g. 27.51 for 27.51%). Leave blank to auto-compute.
NOTE: All fields are optional — the tool analyzes whatever you provide. More fields = more accurate verdict. At minimum, fill revenue (3 years), gross profit, operating income, net income, EBITDA, cash, debt, OCF, and CapEx for a meaningful result.
